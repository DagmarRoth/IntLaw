The Courtroom at the Center of Everything

How domestic courts became the last line of international justice

By Dagmar Rothschild

The plane touched down in Tripoli just hours after the man inside it should have been transferred to The Hague. On January 21, 2025, Osama Almasri Najim, a former head of Libyan judicial police suspected of murder, torture, enslavement, and sexual violence at the notorious Mitiga prison, walked off an Italian government aircraft as a free man. But he was supposed to be in the custody of the International Criminal Court who had released him two days after his arrest in a Turin hotel. The warrant sitting in ICC files went unfulfilled.

It was, said Cristina Orsini of Lawyers for Justice in Libya, a blow to the Rome Statute, the governing doctrine of the ICC. It was also something else: a data point in a longer, quieter story about where international justice actually lives.

The answer, increasingly, is in domestic courtrooms.
